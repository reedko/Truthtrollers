The SAT is owned by a non-profit called The College Board, whose revenue is around $1.2 billion. The CEO makes $2 million a year and several executives are around $300K-$1.2 million. Non profit does not mean non-money.  
There's a secondary billion-dollar test prep industry of companies like Kaplan. 
Who pays?  For public schools, the state pays The College Board (tax payers).
Who benefits? Only those who are going to a college that requires it and there are very few who do.  
So why do schools care?  Well, GreatSchools ranks nearly every K-12 school in the US and one of their data points is the SAT.  Real estate companies like Redfin or Zillow use GreatSchools ratings on their postings. Neighborhoods with highly rated schools attract richer folks, which, in turn, increases property taxes. See where this is going?  When a student opts out, their score is recorded as a zero. This pulls down the school’s average SAT ranking, which can negatively impact local property values (and taxes) and lead to declining enrollment for that school. 
In addition, schools may receive severe consequences if they have low participation rates.
